The baseline is trained only on answerable examples, while the abstain-aware
variant includes explicit supervision for unanswerable cases.
For Stage 1, a win is defined conservatively: unsupported-answer rate should
decrease, answerable performance should remain usable, and abstain quality
should improve without the model collapsing into trivial refusal behavior.
Under that conservative criterion, the current Stage 1 result is a qualified
success.
The abstain-aware model cuts unsupported-answer rate by 72.03 points, from
100.00\% to 27.97\%, and raises decision-aware overall $F_1$ by 27.05 points,
from 42.35 to 69.40.
At the same time, answerable $F_1$ drops by 18.07 points, from 84.82 to 66.76,
so the improvement comes with a real answer-quality cost rather than a free
gain.

Because the abstain-aware system uses a validation-selected threshold, the main
table should be read together with a threshold sweep rather than as a single
isolated operating point.

\paragraph{Threshold interpretation.}
Figure~\ref{fig:stage1-threshold-sweep} shows the expected trade-off as the
threshold moves toward less negative values.
Across the dev sweep from $-3.5$ to $-1.5$, answerable $F_1$ rises from 63.06
to 69.47, but unsupported-answer rate also rises from 24.71\% to 31.77\%.
Abstain $F_1$ changes more gently, moving from 73.39 to 71.86 across the same
range.
The selected threshold of $-2.5$ sits in the middle of this trade-off region:
neighboring points at $-3.0$ and $-2.0$ have similar dev overall $F_1$
(69.29 and 69.17 versus 69.40 at $-2.5$), which suggests that the gain is not
coming from a single brittle threshold setting.

\paragraph{Stage 1 takeaway.}
The main question for Stage 1 was whether explicit abstention supervision could
correct the baseline's pathological always-answer behavior.
On that question, the answer is yes.
The baseline answers every unsupported example, while the abstain-aware model
learns a meaningful abstain policy and achieves 72.98 abstain $F_1$ on the dev
split.
The cost is that answer extraction quality remains substantially worse than the
baseline on answerable questions, which gives a clear target for Stage 2 and
later stages: preserve the abstention gain while recovering answer quality.
