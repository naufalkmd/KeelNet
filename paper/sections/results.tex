The baseline is trained only on answerable examples, while the abstain-aware
variant includes explicit supervision for unanswerable cases.
For Stage 1, a win is defined conservatively: unsupported-answer rate should
decrease, answerable performance should remain usable, and abstain quality
should improve without the model collapsing into trivial refusal behavior.
Under that conservative criterion, the current Stage 1 result is a qualified
success.
The abstain-aware model cuts unsupported-answer rate by 72.03 points, from
100.00\% to 27.97\%, and raises decision-aware overall $F_1$ by 27.05 points,
from 42.35 to 69.40.
At the same time, answerable $F_1$ drops by 18.07 points, from 84.82 to 66.76,
so the improvement comes with a real answer-quality cost rather than a free
gain.

Because the abstain-aware system uses a validation-selected threshold, the main
table should be read together with a threshold sweep rather than as a single
isolated operating point.

\paragraph{Threshold interpretation.}
Figure~\ref{fig:stage1-threshold-sweep} shows the expected trade-off as the
threshold moves toward less negative values.
Across the dev sweep from $-3.5$ to $-1.5$, answerable $F_1$ rises from 63.06
to 69.47, but unsupported-answer rate also rises from 24.71\% to 31.77\%.
Abstain $F_1$ changes more gently, moving from 73.39 to 71.86 across the same
range.
The selected threshold of $-2.5$ sits in the middle of this trade-off region:
neighboring points at $-3.0$ and $-2.0$ have similar dev overall $F_1$
(69.29 and 69.17 versus 69.40 at $-2.5$), which suggests that the gain is not
coming from a single brittle threshold setting.

\paragraph{Stage 1 takeaway.}
The main question for Stage 1 was whether explicit abstention supervision could
correct the baseline's pathological always-answer behavior.
On that question, the answer is yes.
The baseline answers every unsupported example, while the abstain-aware model
learns a meaningful abstain policy and achieves 72.98 abstain $F_1$ on the dev
split.
The cost is that answer extraction quality remains substantially worse than the
baseline on answerable questions, which gives a clear target for Stage 2 and
later stages: preserve the abstention gain while recovering answer quality.

\paragraph{Stage 2 support-verification result.}
Stage 2 keeps the Stage 1 abstain-aware QA model fixed and adds a verifier over
question, passage, and predicted answer.
The selected support threshold is 0.25.
On the validation split, the verifier reaches 87.71 support $F_1$.
On the held-out development split, it reaches 79.79 support $F_1$, with a
66.26\% supported-answer rate and a 33.00\% contradiction rate.
When that support signal is used to gate the Stage 1 abstain-aware system, the
end-to-end QA metrics move only slightly: decision-aware overall $F_1$ rises
from 69.40 to 69.42 and unsupported-answer rate falls from 27.97\% to 27.57\%,
while answerable $F_1$ slips from 66.76 to 66.41.

\paragraph{How to read the Stage 2 outcome.}
The positive result is that the verifier does learn a meaningful support signal
instead of collapsing completely.
The limiting result is that this signal does not yet translate into a large
downstream improvement.
On the dev split, verifier recall is extremely high at 99.43\%, but precision
is only 66.62\%, and the model predicts support for 98.88\% of answered cases.
That makes the current gating policy too permissive: the verifier mostly
confirms answers rather than filtering unsupported ones.
As a result, Stage 2 should be read as a systems success but not yet as a
decisive end-to-end win.
It narrows the next bottleneck clearly: later stages need better calibration
and control over when support scores are allowed to suppress or permit answers.

\paragraph{Representative failure cases.}
The saved dev predictions make the current bottleneck concrete.
On the unanswerable question \emph{``What is France a region of?''}, the Stage 1
baseline answers \emph{``Normandy''}, the Stage 1 abstain-aware model still
answers \emph{``Normandy''}, and Stage 2 assigns that answer a support score of
0.9998 and keeps the final decision as \texttt{answer}.
This is a clean false-positive support error: the passage contains both
\emph{France} and \emph{Normandy}, but the relation in the question is reversed
relative to the evidence.
By contrast, on the answerable question \emph{``If input size is equal to n,
what can respectively be assumed is the function of n?''}, the Stage 1
abstain-aware model correctly predicts \emph{``the time taken''}, but Stage 2
assigns that answer a support score of only 0.0052 and gates it to
\abstain.
This is the complementary false-negative support error:
the verifier can over-penalize short abstract answers even when the QA model is
already correct.
Taken together, these examples match the aggregate metrics:
the current verifier is strong enough to learn support as a signal, but not yet
selective enough to improve the final decision reliably.
