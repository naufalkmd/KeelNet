\paragraph{Stage 1 takeaway.}
The main question for Stage 1 was whether explicit abstention supervision could
correct the baseline's pathological always-answer behavior.
On that question, the answer is yes.

The cost is that answer extraction quality remains substantially worse than the
baseline on answerable questions, which gives a clear target for Stage 2 and
later stages: preserve the abstention gain while recovering answer quality.

\paragraph{Stage 2 support-verification result.}
Stage 2 keeps the Stage 1 abstain-aware QA model fixed and adds a verifier over
question, passage, and predicted answer.
The selected support threshold is 0.25.
On the validation split, the verifier reaches 87.71 support $F_1$.
On the held-out development split, it reaches 79.79 support $F_1$, with a
66.26\% supported-answer rate and a 33.00\% contradiction rate.
When that support signal is used to gate the Stage 1 abstain-aware system, the
end-to-end QA metrics move only slightly: decision-aware overall $F_1$ rises
from 69.40 to 69.42 and unsupported-answer rate falls from 27.97\% to 27.57\%,
while answerable $F_1$ slips from 66.76 to 66.41.

\paragraph{Stage 2.5 hard-negative repair.}
Stage 2.5 keeps the same Stage 1 abstain-aware base model and the same gated
evaluation rule, but retrains the verifier after augmenting its training data
with answered Stage 1 QA predictions as hard positives and hard negatives.
In the current run, this adds 72{,}184 QA-derived verifier examples to the
train split, 7{,}606 to validation, and 6{,}084 to the held-out dev-side
verification set.
The repair does make the verifier slightly less permissive:
dev support precision rises from 66.62\% to 67.05\%, predicted-supported rate
falls from 98.88\% to 97.07\%, and contradiction rate falls from 33.00\% to
31.99\%.
The downstream gated unsupported-answer rate also drops from 27.57\% to
26.95\%.
However, support $F_1$ barely changes (79.79 to 79.70), answerable $F_1$
falls from 66.41 to 65.62, and gated overall $F_1$ slips from 69.42 to 69.34.
The selected support threshold also moves sharply upward, from 0.25 to 0.95,
which indicates that the repaired verifier still needs an aggressive cutoff to
avoid over-trusting answered predictions.

\paragraph{How to read the Stage 2 and Stage 2.5 outcome.}
The positive result is that the verifier does learn a meaningful support signal
instead of collapsing completely.
The limiting result is that neither the original verifier nor the hard-negative
repair translates into a large downstream improvement.
Stage 2 clearly over-confirms answers, and Stage 2.5 softens that tendency only
slightly.
The repaired verifier is directionally better on precision and contradiction
rate, but the gains are too small to recover answerable quality or to produce
a convincing end-to-end jump.
So Stage 2.5 should be read as a useful repair attempt rather than as a new
stage that overturns the earlier diagnosis.
It confirms that easy-negative training data was part of the problem, but not
the whole problem.
The larger bottleneck remains the same:
later stages need stronger control over how support scores are used, or a
different learning objective altogether.

\paragraph{Representative failure cases.}
The saved dev predictions make the current bottleneck concrete.
On the unanswerable question \emph{``What is France a region of?''}, the Stage 1
baseline answers \emph{``Normandy''}, the Stage 1 abstain-aware model still
answers \emph{``Normandy''}, and Stage 2 assigns that answer a support score of
0.9998 and keeps the final decision as \texttt{answer}.
This is a clean false-positive support error: the passage contains both
\emph{France} and \emph{Normandy}, but the relation in the question is reversed
relative to the evidence.
By contrast, on the answerable question \emph{``If input size is equal to n,
what can respectively be assumed is the function of n?''}, the Stage 1
abstain-aware model correctly predicts \emph{``the time taken''}, but Stage 2
assigns that answer a support score of only 0.0052 and gates it to
\abstain.
This is the complementary false-negative support error:
the verifier can over-penalize short abstract answers even when the QA model is
already correct.
Stage 2.5 reduces the aggregate permissiveness of the verifier slightly, but it
does not remove this qualitative pattern.
Taken together, these examples match the aggregate metrics:
the current verifier is strong enough to learn support as a signal, but not yet
selective enough to improve the final decision reliably.

\paragraph{Stage 3 calibration result.}
Stage 3 applies scalar temperature scaling to two score streams without
retraining the underlying models:
the Stage 1 answer-versus-\abstain\ decision score and the Stage 2 support
score.
Both selected temperatures hit the top of the searched range,
\(T_{\mathrm{qa}} = 5.0\) and \(T_{\mathrm{sup}} = 5.0\), which is itself a
useful finding.
It indicates that both score distributions were substantially too sharp before
calibration, especially the verifier.
On the dev split, the raw QA signal has mean confidence 0.9458 against actual
decision accuracy 0.7002, while the raw support signal has mean confidence
0.9873 against actual support accuracy 0.6665.
So before calibration, both modules are strongly overconfident rather than only
slightly misaligned.

\paragraph{What improved.}
The QA signal responds well to this simple calibration step.
Dev QA ECE falls from 0.2456 to 0.0753, MCE falls from 0.3289 to 0.1019,
Brier score improves from 0.2631 to 0.2009, and the threshold-gap measure
falls from 0.1122 to 0.0890.
The support signal also improves materially:
ECE drops from 0.3326 to 0.1708, adaptive ECE from 0.3247 to 0.1686, MCE from
0.8838 to 0.2883, Brier score from 0.3324 to 0.2483, and threshold gap from
0.1317 to 0.0894.
Figure~\ref{fig:stage3-reliability} shows the same pattern qualitatively.
Both calibrated curves move closer to the diagonal, and the extreme
near-certainty behavior of the raw support signal is softened substantially.

\paragraph{What did not improve enough.}
Stage 3 is not yet a new downstream controller, so the end-to-end answer and
abstain metrics remain the Stage 2 ones at this stage.
The key question is therefore not whether overall QA \(F_1\) changed already,
but whether the confidence signals became good enough to drive a stronger fixed
policy in Stage 4.
On that question, the answer is mixed.
The calibrated QA signal now looks reasonably usable.
The calibrated support signal is better than before, but it is still weaker
than QA on every reported calibration metric and its correlation with the true
support label remains low at 0.1296.
So Stage 3 succeeds as a post-hoc calibration study, but it also confirms that
support confidence remains the fragile part of the pipeline.

\paragraph{Stage 3 takeaway.}
The main finding from Stage 3 is not that temperature scaling solves grounded
QA by itself.
The main finding is that the earlier Stage 2 bottleneck was real:
the verifier's scores were badly overconfident and needed flattening before
they could be trusted by later stages.
Simple scalar calibration already fixes much of that overconfidence, but the
support signal still lags the QA decision signal after calibration.
This sharpens the next-stage objective considerably:
Stage 4 should not merely add another heuristic filter, but should combine the
now-better-calibrated QA and support signals into a tighter unsupported-answer
controller and test whether that finally produces a meaningful end-to-end gain.
The later Stage 2.5 hard-negative repair slightly reduces verifier
permissiveness, but it does not materially change this interpretation.
Read together, Stages 1, 2, 2.5, and 3 therefore support a broader
methodological point:
modular post-hoc tuning can improve grounded QA behavior, but in this setup it
does not yet close the objective gap between answer correctness, evidence
support, and abstention quality.
That is the empirical reason to compare future modular controllers against a
direct support-constrained learning formulation rather than continuing to add
modules without changing the learning target.

\paragraph{Stage 4 fixed-controller result.}
Stage 4 uses the calibrated QA and support signals from Stage 3 and searches a
fixed controller under a nominal 20\% unsupported-answer budget on validation.
The selected validation configuration uses support threshold 0.40, QA
threshold 0.40, joint threshold 0.45, and \(\alpha = 0.90\).
On the validation split, this configuration satisfies the budget with
14.30\% unsupported-answer rate and reaches 72.43 decision-aware overall
\(F_1\).
On the held-out development split, however, the controller reaches 66.41
answerable \(F_1\), 69.41 decision-aware overall \(F_1\), 27.59\%
unsupported-answer rate, 72.96 abstain \(F_1\), 66.61\% supported-answer rate
among answered cases, and a 50.68\% answer rate.

\paragraph{How to read the Stage 4 result.}
The key finding is that calibration clearly improves the quality of the
confidence signals, but the first fixed controller does not turn those better
signals into a better development-time decision rule.
Relative to the original Stage 2 gated system, answerable \(F_1\) is
unchanged at 66.41, overall \(F_1\) slips by 0.01 points from 69.42 to 69.41,
unsupported-answer rate worsens by 0.02 points from 27.57\% to 27.59\%, and
abstain \(F_1\) also slips by 0.01 points.
Supported-answer rate among answered cases and answer rate are likewise almost
identical.
So Stage 4 should be read as a useful null result:
the calibrated controller is validation-feasible, but on development it is
effectively indistinguishable from the simpler Stage 2 gate.
That makes the modular story sharper, not weaker:
calibration matters for score quality, but the first fixed controller still
fails to leverage that better score behavior into a meaningful end-to-end
gain.

\paragraph{Stage 5 standalone result.}
Stage 5 is intended to compare a jointly trained support-constrained learner
against the executed modular controller family.
That comparison is now available directly: Stage 4 provides the intended
calibrated-controller baseline, and the original Stage 2 gate remains the
slightly stronger modular checkpoint on development by 0.01 overall \(F_1\).
In the current run, the selected keep threshold is 0.65.
On the held-out development split, the learner reaches 71.40 answerable
$F_1$, 68.28 decision-aware overall $F_1$, 34.84\% unsupported-answer rate,
71.17 abstain $F_1$, 62.40\% supported-answer rate among answered cases, and a
58.38\% answer rate.
The run is also evaluated under a nominal 20\% unsupported-answer budget, which
it misses by 14.84 points.

\paragraph{What Stage 5 improved and what it worsened.}
The positive result is clear:
the support-constrained learner substantially recovers answerable-question
quality.
Relative to the Stage 1 abstain-aware model, answerable $F_1$ rises by 4.64
points, from 66.76 to 71.40.
Relative to the Stage 2.5 hard-negative gated system, it rises by 5.78 points,
from 65.62 to 71.40.
However, the groundedness side moves in the wrong direction.
Unsupported-answer rate rises from 27.97\% in Stage 1 to 34.84\% in Stage 5,
and from 26.95\% in Stage 2.5 to 34.84\%.
That increase is large enough that overall $F_1$ falls below every previously
executed modular checkpoint, including the 69.41 reached by the calibrated
Stage 4 controller and the slightly higher 69.42 reached by the original
Stage 2 gated system.

\paragraph{How to read the current Stage 5 finding.}
This first Stage 5 run is therefore a useful negative result rather than a
successful replacement for the modular pipeline.
Changing the learning target does matter:
the learner moves the trade-off strongly and recovers answerable extraction
quality that the abstain-aware and verifier-gated systems had given up.
But the current objective and thresholding setup do not yet bind unsupported
answering tightly enough.
So the direct learner is not automatically safer just because support and
abstention are trained jointly.
What it can claim already is sharper:
the first direct support-constrained objective exposes a different failure mode
than the modular pipeline, namely that answer quality can be recovered faster
than unsupported-answer control.

\paragraph{Stage 6 adaptive revision result.}
Stage 6 is designed as a Stage 5 revision rather than as a new standalone
pipeline:
it reuses the trained Stage 5 learner to generate candidate features, then
learns an adaptive balancer over those candidate-level signals.
The fairest immediate comparison is still Stage 6 against Stage 5, but the run
can now also be read against the executed modular baselines, including Stage 4.
In the current run, the selected candidate threshold is 0.50.
On the held-out development split, Stage 6 reaches 69.70 answerable $F_1$,
67.62 decision-aware overall $F_1$, 34.45\% unsupported-answer rate, 71.12
abstain $F_1$, 62.41\% supported-answer rate among answered cases, and a
57.77\% answer rate.
Under the same nominal 20\% unsupported-answer budget used for Stage 5, it
still misses the target by 14.45 points.

\paragraph{What Stage 6 changed and what it did not.}
Relative to Stage 5, the adaptive revision makes the system only slightly more
conservative.
Answer rate falls from 58.38\% to 57.77\%, and unsupported-answer rate falls by
0.39 points, from 34.84\% to 34.45\%.
But the gain is too small to matter at the system level:
supported-answer rate among kept answers is effectively unchanged, moving only
from 62.40\% to 62.41\%, while answerable $F_1$ falls by 1.70 points and
overall $F_1$ falls by 0.66 points.
So the first adaptive learner trims answering slightly without materially
improving groundedness.

\paragraph{How to read the current Stage 6 finding.}
The first Stage 6 run is therefore a second informative negative result rather
than a breakthrough.
It shows that adding candidate-level adaptive balancing on top of the current
Stage 5 pipeline is not sufficient by itself.
The gap is no longer only that the first direct learner is too permissive; it
is also that the first adaptive correction changes behavior too weakly to
recover the unsupported-answer budget or beat the simpler Stage 5 learner.
What it can claim already is narrower and still useful:
the current Stage 6 mechanism behaves mostly like a slightly more cautious
Stage 5, not yet like a new frontier point on the
utility-versus-groundedness trade-off.

\paragraph{Stage 7 risk-budgeted action result.}
Stage 7 changes the formulation more aggressively than Stage 6.
Instead of thresholding independently scored candidates, it treats candidate
answers and \abstain\ as one action space and learns separate utility and risk
signals under explicit unsupported-answer and over-abstention budgets.
In the current run, the selected risk threshold is 0.85.
On the validation split, that operating point satisfies both target budgets:
unsupported-answer rate is 19.22\% against a 20\% maximum, over-abstention rate
is 12.92\% against a 20\% maximum, overall $F_1$ is 73.06, and answerable
$F_1$ is 69.19.
On the held-out development split, however, the same operating point reaches
69.95 answerable $F_1$, 67.66 decision-aware overall $F_1$,
34.62\% unsupported-answer rate, 71.11 abstain $F_1$, 62.28\%
supported-answer rate among answered cases, 57.99\% answer rate, and
18.57\% over-abstention rate.

\paragraph{What Stage 7 changed and what it did not.}
Relative to Stage 6, Stage 7 improves answerable $F_1$ only slightly, from
69.70 to 69.95, and overall $F_1$ only marginally, from 67.62 to 67.66.
At the same time, unsupported-answer rate worsens slightly, from 34.45\% to
34.62\%, supported-answer rate among answered cases slips from 62.41\% to
62.28\%, and answer rate rises slightly from 57.77\% to 57.99\%.
So the action learner does not win simply by refusing more often.
Its over-abstention rate stays within the nominal 20\% budget, but the
unsupported-answer budget still fails on development by 14.62 points.
Relative to Stage 5, Stage 7 is slightly less permissive
(34.62\% versus 34.84\% unsupported-answer rate), but it still gives back
1.45 points of answerable $F_1$ and 0.62 points of overall $F_1$.

\paragraph{How to read the current Stage 7 finding.}
The first Stage 7 run is therefore a third informative negative result rather
than the long-sought clean direct-learning win.
The positive result is that the stronger formulation really does solve the
\emph{validation} problem it was designed for:
explicit \abstain, explicit risk, and explicit budgets are enough to select an
operating point that satisfies both validation constraints.
The limiting result is that this gain does not transfer to the held-out
development split, where unsupported-answer rate remains essentially unchanged
from the Stage 5--6 regime.
So the current bottleneck is no longer just the lack of an explicit action
space.
It is the lack of a risk signal whose supported-versus-unsupported separation
generalizes beyond validation-time threshold selection.

\paragraph{Stage 8 Hybrid result.}
Stage 8 returns to a modular combination:
it keeps Stage 5 as the answer engine and adds calibrated Stage 4 control
signals back on top.
In the current run, the selected candidate threshold is 0.20.
On the held-out development split, Stage 8 reaches 68.39 answerable $F_1$,
68.27 decision-aware overall $F_1$, 31.86\% unsupported-answer rate, 71.71
abstain $F_1$, 64.27\% supported-answer rate among answered cases, and a
54.91\% answer rate.

\paragraph{What Stage 8 Hybrid changed and what it did not.}
Relative to the Stage 5--7 family, the hybrid is meaningfully more
conservative.
Unsupported-answer rate falls by 2.98 points relative to Stage 5, by
2.59 points relative to Stage 6, and by 2.76 points relative to Stage 7.
That groundedness gain also lifts overall $F_1$ above the Stage 5--7 regime,
with a 0.61-point improvement over Stage 7.
But the recovery is still incomplete:
answerable $F_1$ remains 3.01 points below Stage 5, and the hybrid still
trails the calibrated Stage 4 controller by 1.14 overall $F_1$ and 4.27 points
of unsupported-answer rate.
So restoring calibrated control helps, but it does not yet reclaim the modular
frontier.

\paragraph{Stage 8.2 clean-split action-learner result.}
Stage 8.2 keeps Stage 5 as the answer engine, injects calibrated Stage 4
support into the Stage 7 action learner, and reports its final checkpoint on a
truly untouched test split.
In the current run, the selected risk threshold is 0.70.
On validation, that operating point satisfies the nominal unsupported-answer
budget with 19.31\% unsupported-answer rate and 72.98 overall $F_1$.
On the final test split, however, it reaches 70.06 answerable $F_1$,
68.16 decision-aware overall $F_1$, 33.73\% unsupported-answer rate, 71.09
abstain $F_1$, 63.28\% supported-answer rate among answered cases, and a
56.72\% answer rate.
So the cleaned formulation reproduces the same central pattern under stricter
evaluation hygiene:
the controller can satisfy the budget on validation, but the
supported-versus-unsupported separation still does not transfer tightly enough
to the final held-out split.

\paragraph{Final chosen learned architecture.}
The paper's final chosen learned architecture is therefore Stage 8.2, not
because it is the strongest final operating point, but because it is the
cleanest executed deep-learning formulation of the full hypothesis.
It freezes the Stage 5 DistilBERT answer engine as a proposal network,
rescoring the top candidate spans with a shallow action model that receives
both answer-quality features and calibrated Stage 4 support features.
That action model learns separate utility and risk heads for each candidate,
plus a pooled abstain head over the whole candidate set.
This architecture is the closest executed system to a true
support-aware selective predictor:
the final answer-or-\abstain\ choice is learned directly over a compact action
space, under explicit unsupported-answer budgets, while still preserving a
modular calibrated support signal.
The empirical result is still sobering.
Figure~\ref{fig:final-comparison-frontier} shows that the architecture moves
the learned family toward better answerable \(F_1\), but not yet to a safer
overall frontier than Stage 4.
